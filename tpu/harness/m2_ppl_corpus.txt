Angular momentum is a fundamental quantity in classical and quantum mechanics. For a single particle with respect to a fixed point, it is defined as the cross product of the position vector and the linear momentum vector. In an isolated system where no external torque acts, angular momentum is conserved, and this conservation law underlies the stability of orbits, the spin of subatomic particles, and the rotation of galaxies. The total angular momentum of a rigid body is computed by integrating the contribution of each mass element, and for symmetric bodies this reduces to a product of the moment of inertia and the angular velocity. In quantum mechanics, angular momentum is quantized in units of the reduced Planck constant, and only certain discrete values of its magnitude and projection along a chosen axis are measurable. Operators for the components of angular momentum do not commute with each other, which leads to the uncertainty relations characteristic of quantum systems. The eigenstates of angular momentum form a complete basis for the rotational degree of freedom of a particle moving in a central potential, and the spherical harmonics are the angular parts of these eigenfunctions. When two angular momenta are coupled, the resulting total can take values from their sum down to the absolute value of their difference, in integer steps, and the Clebsch-Gordan coefficients describe how product states decompose into eigenstates of the total angular momentum.
